Rounding Is a Choice

April 4, 2026 math

Most people learn one rounding rule: if the fractional part is exactly $0.5$, round up. It feels obvious. It is also systematically wrong.

Consider five values, each sitting exactly at a half:

$$0.5,\quad 1.5,\quad 2.5,\quad 3.5,\quad 4.5$$

Their true sum is $12.5$. Round each one up and you get $1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 = 15$. The rounded sum overshoots by $2.5$ — half a unit per value. This isn't bad luck. Round-half-up always breaks ties in the same direction, so any dataset with values near a half accumulates upward bias at a predictable rate.

Formally, if $X$ is uniform on $[n - 0.5,\, n + 0.5)$ for integer $n$, round-half-up gives:

$$\mathbb{E}\bigl[\text{round}_{\uparrow}(X) - X\bigr] = \frac{1}{4n+2} > 0$$

Small, but nonzero, and it compounds.

The fix

Banker's rounding — also called round-half-to-even — breaks ties toward the nearest even integer instead. So $0.5 \to 0$, $1.5 \to 2$, $2.5 \to 2$, $3.5 \to 4$, $4.5 \to 4$.

Applied to the same five values: $0 + 2 + 2 + 4 + 4 = 12$. Still not exactly $12.5$ — rounding always loses information — but the error is $-0.5$ rather than $+2.5$, and crucially, the expected error over many such rounds is zero:

$$\mathbb{E}\bigl[\text{round}_{\text{even}}(X) - X\bigr] = 0$$

Half the tie-breaks go down, half go up. The bias cancels.

It compounds

Rounding rarely happens once. When a rounded value feeds into a later calculation that itself gets rounded, errors carry. Let $\epsilon_i$ be the rounding error at step $i$. Under round-half-up, $\mathbb{E}[\epsilon_i] > 0$ at every step, so after $n$ steps the expected accumulated error is $O(n)$. Under banker's rounding, $\mathbb{E}[\epsilon_i] = 0$ and errors partially cancel, giving accumulated error $O(\sqrt{n})$ — the difference between a drift and a random walk.

Why it matters

IEEE 754 — the floating point standard governing virtually every calculator, spreadsheet, and programming language — mandates banker's rounding as its default. Actuarial and financial calculations that aggregate many rounded figures rely on this property to keep totals consistent. The rule that feels natural is the one with the worst long-run behavior. The fix is a single policy change that costs nothing.

Rounding is not a mechanical fact. It is a choice, and the default choice is quietly biased.
